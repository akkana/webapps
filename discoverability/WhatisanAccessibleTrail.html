What is an Accessible Trail?

Terry Foxx

I use a Rollator Walker for walking trails and getting to my perching places outside. For the past 6 months I have been walking various trails and some I have found are not for the Rollator! Others are! My question was “How do I write up those trails that I can only access a short distance.” The legal documents were a little too cumbersome for me and too detailed.

So what makes a trail accessible to those that use Rollator Walkers and Wheelchairs? I found a summary article on the web that really put it in simple language. Beyond that, these three elements were ones I had also found, from my experience, to be important. They are trail width, cross slope (side to side grade) and obstacles.

Trail Width

My Drive Rollator Walker has a 23-inch base. When I find a trail that is only 2 feet wide often, I must put one wheel on the packed soil and the other on the vegetation surrounding the trail. This causes the Rollator Walker to be tilted and more difficult to maneuver. If the packed soil is several inches deep from feet, animals, or erosion the tilting is very difficult and unstable.


If the trail is at least 36 inches, I have no problem particularly if the area is paved or packed soil and has no major obstacles. Sand, mud, or erosion can make it more difficult to push the Rollator.

Cross Slope:

Unfortunately, I have most often measured the grade of the slope. I have found the grade in percent is important. Pushing a walker uphill takes effort and steep grades are very tiring. The recommendations are the following:


Up to 8.33% for 200 ft (one needs to rest about every 200 ft when using a Rollator)

Up to 10% for 30 feet with resting intervals every 30 ft

12.5% for 10 feet with resting intervals every 10 ft.


I have only begun to measure the cross slope which should be no more that 5%.


No matter how the slope is one must keep in mind that if you go down a slope, in most cases, you must push the walker up-slope which is a challenge.

Obstacles

Obstacles with a trail can include rocks and tree roots. They should be no more than two inches high. But in many locations the prevalence of rocks is a major barrier. One or two rocks in a trail, that are no more than 2-3 inches high, can usually be straddled with the Rollator Walker but in our area many trails are very rocky.


There are many trails I have accessed with one or two of these elements, but usually only for a distance of no more than ½ mile or less. But if they have any of these elements, I have classified them as inaccessible to many Rollator Walker users. As a result we have in the area 6-7 very accessible trails which is very encouraging.
